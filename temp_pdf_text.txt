MicroscopyA B S T R A C T
Aperture-scanning Fourier ptychography is a configuration of Fourier ptychgraphy by scanning an aperture at
the back focal plane of the objective lens. It has been a useful tool for measuring the complex scattering field
exited from a sample. However, in this method the intensity of dark images at the charge coupled device (CCD)
plane is of same order or even lower than the noise when the high frequency of the Fourier spectrum is filtered
by the aperture. Therefore, the recorded dark field images suffer from low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), which
results in poor reconstruction. In this paper, we report a modified Fourier ptychographic imaging method by
wavefront modulation. Instead of an aperture, a diffuser is used to modulate the spectrum in the presented
method. The complex distribution of the sample and the diffuser are reconstructed simultaneously via iterative
phase retrieval. Numerical simulations and proof-of-concept experiments have been carried out to demonstrate
the feasibility of the presented method.
1. Introduction
Fourier ptychography [1–13] is a newly developed powerful com-
putational imaging framework based on the principles of synthetic
aperture imaging [14–16] and iterative phase retrieval [17–21]. Since
its first proposal, Fourier ptychography has been successfully demon-
strated in the field of high-resolution imaging [22–24], quantitative
phase imaging [25,26] and biomedical applications [27,28]. In the
early implement of Fourier ptychography, a light emitting diode (LED)
array is used to generate beams of varying angles [1] to illuminate
the sample to be observed, and a CCD camera is placed at the image
plane of the imaging system to capture the low resolution images
which is filtered by the numerical aperture (NA). By assuming that the
spectrum of the sample shifts in the Fourier space when the sample
is illuminated by a titled illumination beam, the complex amplitude
of the spectrum can be reconstructed rapidly and accurately through
a common Fourier ptychographic algorithm relying on overlapping
constraints and intensity constraints, hence the high resolution am-
plitude as well as the quantitative phase image of the sample can
be obtained by inverse Fourier transform the reconstructed spectrum.
However, the assumption is established under conditions where the
sample is sufficiently thin, for thick sample the complex distribution
of the spectrum changes when the sample is illuminated by a beam
with different angle, thus the overlapping constraints are not satisfied
and the common ptychographic algorithms will not be able to recon-
struct the complex spectral. To address the thick-limitation problem in
Fourier ptychography, Fourier ptychographic tomography (FPT) [29]

Aperture-scanning Fourier ptychography [34–37] is another method
to bypass the limitation of sample thickness in Fourier ptychography.
Instead of illuminating sample with varying angle beam, in the config-
uration of aperture-scanning Fourier ptychography, the thick sample
is illuminated by a collimated plane wave. An aperture is placed at
the confocal plane of the 4f imaging system to modulate the spectrum
of the thick sample and multiple intensity images including the bright
field images and dark field images are recorded by a CCD (or CMOS)
camera when the aperture is shifting in the confocal plane. After
data acquisition is completed, the recorded images are converted to
the complex distribution of the spectrum by a conventional Fourier
ptychographic algorithm [38,39] to recover the wavefront exited from
the thick sample. Due to its convenient setup and low computational
complexity, aperture-scanning Fourier ptychography has been used for
phase imaging [36], topography [40], 3D refocusing, and pixel super-
resolution [34,41]. However, the intensity of dark image at the CCD
plane is of same order or even lower than the noise when the aperture
is scanned to the high frequency position of the spectrum, hence
the recorded dark field images suffer from low signal-to-noise ratio
(SNR), resulting in poor reconstruction of aperture-scanning Fourier
ptychography.
Recently, a diffuser was employed in imaging methods based on
phase retrieval. Zhang et al. [21] proposed a phase retrieval method by

coherent modulation imaging, in which a diffuser is inserted between
the object and detector to modulate the wavefield. The method has
proven to have the advantages of compact setup, fast data acquisition,
and high convergence speed. However, in their method, the complex
transmission function of the diffuser should be known accurately in ad-
vance. Xie et al. [42] proposed an improved scanning-aperture Fourier
ptychographic method by placing a known phase modulator in the
optical setup. The method was demonstrated with better convergence
and robust to shift errors and noise by computer simulations, however
the method still needs to scan the spectrum in the confocal plane
using an aperture, the problem of low SNR of the dark field images
were not avoided. In addition, it is difficult to accurately obtain the
complex distribution of the phase modulator in the experiment. Zheng
et al. [43,44] proposed pattern-illuminated Fourier ptychography and
near field Fourier ptychography by illuminating a sample with shifted
diffuser. However, their method cannot be used for thick sample unless
multiple-slice algorithm [30] is combined.
In this paper, we propose a robust Fourier ptychographic imaging
method via wavefront modulation with a diffuser. Instead of using an
aperture, a diffuser placed near the con-focal plane is fixed on a transla-
tion stage and shifted laterally to modulate the complex spectrum of the
sample in the proposed method. In the reconstruction, the amplitude as
well as the phase of the complex sample and the diffuser are iteratively
reconstructed simultaneously via a Fourier ptychographic algorithm.
The manuscript is organized as follows: Section 2 describes the pro-
posed method in detail; Section 3 shows the simulation results and
Section 4 shows the experimental results to demonstrate the feasibility
of the proposed method; and Section 5 concludes.

In conclusion, we have proposed and experimentally demonstrated
a Fourier ptychographic imaging method via wavefront modulation
with an unknown diffuser. By scanning the diffuser, the complex am-
plitude of the sample and diffuser can be simultaneously reconstructed
in this method, and the proposed method has been demonstrated to
be more robust to the noise compared to the conventional aperture-
scanning Fourier ptychographic method using an aperture. Experimen-
tal results show that the presented method is able to measure the phase
information of the sample being inspected.
It is worth to point out that in conventional aperture-scanning
Fourier ptychography, if a large aperture is used and shifted in the
confocal plane, the low frequency component of the sample will always
passes through the imaging system, and no dark images would be
recorded, thus the recorded images have high SNR. However, in this
case, the recorded images lack data diversity, which will result in slow
convergence and low reconstruction accuracy. In our method, the low
frequency and high frequency components of the sample spectrum are
both modulated at each position of the diffuser, which results in high
data diversity at the detector plane. Therefore, the presented method
has better convergence and high reconstruction accuracy.

In our experimental implement of the method, a holographic dif-
fuser is mechanically scanned which introduces location errors of the
diffuser and increases the data acquisition time, an alternate implement
of the proposed method is to replace the holographic diffuser with a
spatial light modulator (SLM) or a digital micromirror device (DMD)
to create shifted random structures. Our future work in this field

The method will be used for applications such as wavefront sensing,
wafer inspection, and optical testing.
